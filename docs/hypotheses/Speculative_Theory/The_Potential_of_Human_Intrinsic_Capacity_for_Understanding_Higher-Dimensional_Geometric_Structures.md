# **The Potential of Human Intrinsic Capacity for Understanding Higher-Dimensional Geometric Structures**

This report investigates structural parallels between human cognition and large language models (LLMs), the emergence of high-dimensional geometric representations in the brain, and the possibility that humans possess an intrinsic capacity to process geometric structures beyond three dimensions. The analysis is framed within a computational meta‑model in which physical laws function as an execution environment for higher‑order informational processes.

---

# **1. Physical Laws as an Execution Environment for Higher-Order Information**

Modern theoretical physics increasingly models the universe not as a set of material objects governed by immutable rules, but as an **information‑centric computational system** in which physical laws emerge from deeper informational structures. Under this framework, the universe operates as a layered computational architecture:

| Layer | Components | Function |
|-------|------------|----------|
| Information Substrate | Bits, quantum states | Fundamental data and computational basis |
| Physical Execution Layer | Physical laws, constants | Algorithmic constraints and execution rules |
| Emergent Complexity Layer | Atoms, stars, life, AI | Self‑organization and accumulation of structured information |
| Higher‑Order Cognition Layer | Cognitive processing, macro‑level control parameters | Optimization of high‑order informational structure |

In this view, the universe evolves by recursively generating patterns within an informational “code space,” and complex systems arise as mechanisms for increasing structured information density.

# **2. Structural Parallels Between Human Cognition and Large Language Models**

LLMs, particularly transformer‑based architectures, exhibit notable similarities to human neural processing. Attention mechanisms resemble modular neural circuits, and both systems rely on predictive processing.

## **2.1 Predictive Coding and Convergent Optimization**

Human brains continuously predict incoming sensory and linguistic information, minimizing prediction error. LLMs perform an analogous process through next‑token prediction. Both systems reduce uncertainty through iterative optimization, suggesting a shared computational principle.

Neural imaging studies show that LLM embeddings align linearly with human brain activity during language tasks, implying that both systems abstract underlying semantic structure in similar ways.

## **2.2 Geometric Manifolds in Latent Space**

LLMs represent concepts as points in a high‑dimensional vector space. Relationships between concepts form geometric manifolds—smooth, structured surfaces embedded in high‑dimensional space. Human cognition appears to map meaning in a similar manifold‑like structure.

| Feature | Human Cognition | LLMs |
|---------|------------------|------|
| Information Weighting | Focused attention | Attention heads |
| Concept Encoding | Neural population codes | High‑dimensional embeddings |
| Optimization | Free‑energy minimization | Gradient descent |
| Memory Dynamics | Semantic networks | Context windows and attention distribution |

These parallels suggest that both humans and LLMs explore the same underlying **semantic manifold**.

# **3. High-Dimensional Structures in the Human Brain**

Neuroscientific research reveals that the brain constructs geometric structures with dimensionality far exceeding three. Using algebraic topology, researchers have shown that neural networks can form transient structures up to eleven dimensions during information processing.

## **3.1 Multidimensional “Sandcastles” of Neural Activity**

When processing information, neurons form highly organized cliques that assemble into multi‑dimensional shapes. As integration increases, these shapes grow in dimensionality and form cavities—topological signatures of semantic extraction. These structures collapse once processing is complete.

This ability to generate high‑dimensional topology may underlie humans’ capacity to comprehend complex, abstract structures.

# **6. Human Capacity for Higher-Dimensional Geometric Understanding**

Contrary to the assumption that humans are limited to three‑dimensional intuition, experimental evidence shows that people can learn to navigate and reason within four‑dimensional spaces.

## **6.1 Navigation and Rigidity Judgments in 4D**

Participants trained in virtual 4D environments can perform accurate path integration and judge rigidity of rotating 4D objects with precision comparable to 3D tasks. This suggests that human spatial cognition is not inherently bound to three dimensions.

## **6.2 Mathematical Intuition and Higher-Dimensional Objects**

Mathematicians often develop intuitive understanding of complex, high‑dimensional structures without relying solely on algebraic formalism. This ability may stem from the brain’s capacity to generate and manipulate high‑dimensional topological structures.
